**Title:** I redesigned my app's onboarding after realizing users couldn't rate songs they'd never heard — here's what changed

**Body:**

I'm building Taste Roulette — a music discovery app where you get one anonymous song recommendation per day from someone whose taste is different from yours.

The first version of onboarding asked people to swipe on 15-20 songs we picked. The idea was: rate these → we build your taste vector → we find you surprising matches.

Problem: early testers only recognized about 30% of the songs. You can't learn someone's taste from unknowns. They were just guessing.

**New onboarding flow:**

1. Pick 3 genres you love (from a visual grid — no text walls)
2. We show you artists within those genres — pick the ones you actually know
3. Then we show ~10 songs from those artists to calibrate the fine-grained preferences
4. Result: a taste vector built on real familiarity, not random reactions

The hypothesis is that you need a reference point before you can navigate toward something surprising. Common ground first, then unknown territory.

**Why this matters for the product:**
The whole point of the app is to find the "sweet spot" between your taste and a stranger's — different enough to be surprising, not so far you reject it. If the input data is noisy, the matching breaks.

Still in closed beta on Android. Small number of testers right now, but the onboarding completion rate went up noticeably after the redesign.

Happy to talk through the algorithm approach (genre vectors + cosine distance) if anyone's curious.
